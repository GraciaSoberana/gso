> The father’s control over the son was for life. He could imprison his son, scourge, shame and punish him, sell him into slavery up to three times, or have him killed. The son’s position in the community was of no consequence; for instance, though he might be a magistrate, he was still under his father’s authority. The father had more power over his son than a master had over his slaves. It would be unfair, however, to suggest that there was no tender loving care within families in the Roman times. Nevertheless, in this milieu Paul writes instructions to the believing fathers in Ephesus.
> <br />
> <span class="author">Horold Hoerner</span><br ><span class="book">Ephesians, An exegetical commentary</span>
